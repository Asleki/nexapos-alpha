# 2. Definitions

## 2.1 Pre-agricultural food system

A system in which communities obtain food mainly through gathering, hunting, fishing, scavenging, exchange, and environmental management without sustained dependence on cultivated fields or managed breeding populations.

## 2.2 Foraging

The organized search, collection, capture, and processing of wild biological resources.

## 2.3 Broad-spectrum subsistence

A strategy that draws food from many species, habitats, seasons, and acquisition methods rather than depending heavily on a narrow set of resources.

## 2.4 Resource intensification

An increase in labour, skill, technology, or management applied to a resource in order to obtain more reliable or larger returns.

## 2.5 Landscape management

Deliberate modification of vegetation, water, fire regimes, access routes, or animal movement to improve useful resources.

## 2.6 Niche construction

The process by which organisms, including humans, alter environments in ways that change future selection pressures and resource conditions.

## 2.7 Cultivation

Intentional actions that assist the growth of useful plants, including clearing, sowing, transplanting, watering, protecting, and harvesting.

## 2.8 Domestication process

A long-term relationship in which repeated human selection and management alter the traits, reproduction, distribution, or behaviour of living organisms.

## 2.9 Management without domestication

Human control over access, timing, protection, movement, or reproduction that has not yet produced stable domesticated traits.

## 2.10 Sedentism

A tendency toward longer or permanent residence in one locality.

## 2.11 Semi-sedentism

A settlement pattern in which a community occupies a primary location for a substantial part of the year while retaining seasonal movement.

## 2.12 Storage economy

A food system in which stored reserves become central to survival, exchange, planning, power, and risk management.

## 2.13 Surplus

Output remaining after immediate consumption, seed needs, feed needs, social obligations, and expected losses.

## 2.14 Delayed-return system

A system where labour invested now produces benefits after a significant interval, requiring planning, ownership expectations, and future-oriented coordination.

## 2.15 Immediate-return system

A system where labour generally produces benefits quickly and where long-term claims over productive assets are limited.

## 2.16 Seasonality

Predictable variation in resources, weather, labour demand, disease, and mobility across the annual cycle.

## 2.17 Aggregation site

A location where people repeatedly gather for food processing, exchange, ritual, storage, or seasonal resource use.

## 2.18 Territoriality

The defence, regulation, or recognition of access to land, water, food resources, or settlement areas.

## 2.19 Property claim

A socially recognized claim over access, use, transfer, exclusion, or inheritance.

## 2.20 Risk pooling

The sharing of food, labour, storage, information, or reciprocal obligations to reduce exposure to individual shocks.

## 2.21 Reciprocal labour

Work exchanged between households or groups through social obligation rather than immediate wage payment.

## 2.22 Cultivation package

A connected set of practices involving planting material, land preparation, timing, tools, labour, storage, and knowledge.

## 2.23 Domestication syndrome

A recurring group of traits that become more common under sustained human selection and management.

## 2.24 Path dependence

A condition in which earlier choices shape later options, costs, institutions, and expectations.

## 2.25 Agrarian threshold

The point at which cultivated or managed production becomes important enough to reorganize settlement, labour, storage, or social institutions.

## 2.26 Food production commitment

The degree to which a community depends on planned biological production rather than mainly obtaining wild resources.

## 2.27 Mixed subsistence

A livelihood that combines cultivation or herding with gathering, hunting, fishing, exchange, or other activities.

## 2.28 Buffer stock

Reserve food held to protect against shortage, delay, conflict, crop failure, or seasonal gaps.

## 2.29 Carrying capacity

The population or activity level that an environment can sustain under defined practices and conditions.

## 2.30 Effective carrying capacity

The carrying capacity created by actual technology, mobility, storage, exchange, and social organization.

## 2.31 Settlement permanence

The degree to which structures, fields, storage, burial, infrastructure, and social claims bind a community to a place.

## 2.32 Labour calendar

The seasonal schedule of tasks, labour peaks, maintenance, harvesting, processing, and storage.

## 2.33 Resource patch

A spatially concentrated area of useful plants, animals, water, fuel, or materials.

## 2.34 Patch reliability

The predictability of a resource patch across time.

## 2.35 Selection pressure

A condition that makes some traits more likely to persist or reproduce than others.

## 2.36 Cultural selection

Human preference and decision-making that favour particular traits, practices, locations, or organisms.

## 2.37 Ecological feedback

A response in which human actions alter environmental conditions that then influence future human choices.

## 2.38 Social storage

Reliance on relationships, obligations, alliances, and reputation as a reserve that can be called upon during need.

## 2.39 Material storage

Physical preservation of food, seed, feed, tools, or raw materials for later use.

## 2.40 Agrarian lock-in

A condition in which population, institutions, land modification, and stored investment make withdrawal from farming increasingly costly.

# 3. Core Explanations

## 3.1 Agriculture as accumulation

Agriculture emerged through accumulated observations, experiments, management practices, and inherited knowledge rather than a single discovery.

## 3.2 From collection to control

The central transition involved increasing control over timing, location, reproduction, protection, and access.

## 3.3 From immediate to delayed returns

Cultivation shifted labour toward future output, increasing the need for planning and claims over expected benefits.

## 3.4 From mobility to place investment

Storage, fields, water systems, and durable structures increased the value of remaining near a location.

## 3.5 From diversity to managed concentration

Production could increase dependable bulk food while narrowing the range of resources used.

## 3.6 From shared access to stronger claims

Repeated investment in land and storage encouraged rules on access, inheritance, exclusion, and compensation.

## 3.7 From ecological knowledge to selective pressure

Human preferences altered the survival and reproduction of managed populations.

## 3.8 From household decisions to institutions

As scale and surplus grew, coordination increasingly involved communities, custodians, councils, ritual authorities, or emerging political institutions.

## 3.9 From seasonal uncertainty to buffering

Storage, diversification, mobility, and exchange functioned as alternative or complementary buffers.

## 3.10 From reversible experiment to agrarian lock-in

Early trials could be abandoned, but later population, infrastructure, and institutions increased dependence on production.

# 4. Historical Context

## 4.1 Climate and environmental variability

Changing rainfall, temperature, vegetation, water, fire regimes, and animal distributions altered the cost and reliability of food acquisition.

## 4.2 Demographic context

Population density affected competition, mobility, labour availability, disease, and incentives to intensify.

## 4.3 Social context

Kinship, reciprocity, ritual, conflict, and alliance shaped access to resources and labour.

## 4.4 Technological context

Digging, cutting, grinding, transport, containers, fire control, and preservation expanded possible strategies.

## 4.5 Economic context

Surplus, exchange, debt, obligation, and measurement became more important as stored and transferable value increased.

## 4.6 Institutional context

Claims over fields, water, seed, storage, and labour required shared rules and enforcement.

# 5. Transition Principles

## 5.1 No single origin principle

Agriculture emerged through multiple pathways rather than one universal event.

## 5.2 Continuum principle

Foraging, management, cultivation, and domestication form overlapping stages rather than sharp categories.

## 5.3 Risk-first principle

Early production choices often sought reliability and control, not simply maximum output.

## 5.4 Seasonality principle

Seasonal scarcity and abundance strongly shaped storage, mobility, labour, and experimentation.

## 5.5 Investment principle

Delayed-return production required greater planning, protection, property expectations, and coordination.

## 5.6 Feedback principle

Human management changed environments, which changed future incentives and resource distributions.

## 5.7 Population-pressure caution

Population growth can encourage intensification, but is not sufficient as a single explanation.

## 5.8 Local opportunity principle

Agriculture was more likely where useful species, water, soils, climate, and social knowledge aligned.

## 5.9 Mixed-strategy principle

Cultivation commonly coexisted with gathering, hunting, fishing, and exchange.

## 5.10 Institution-building principle

Storage, fields, herds, and irrigation created needs for rules, rights, labour coordination, and dispute resolution.

## 5.11 Trade-off principle

Greater food control could increase labour burden, disease exposure, inequality, and ecological pressure.

## 5.12 Evidence plurality principle

Origins must be reconstructed from multiple evidence types rather than one artifact or indicator.

## 5.13 Non-linearity principle

Communities could adopt, reduce, abandon, or later restore cultivation.

## 5.14 Knowledge accumulation principle

Repeated observation across generations turned environmental familiarity into increasingly deliberate management.

## 5.15 Scale-dependence principle

A practice effective at household scale may produce different effects at settlement or regional scale.

# 6. Periodization Without Fixed Dates

## 6.1 Predominantly wild-resource phase

Food comes mainly from gathering, hunting, fishing, and exchange, though environments may already be managed.

## 6.2 Resource-management phase

Communities deliberately alter landscapes, access, timing, or resource abundance.

## 6.3 Cultivation-experiment phase

Small-scale sowing, transplanting, watering, protection, or tending supplements wild foods.

## 6.4 Mixed-production phase

Cultivation or animal management becomes regular but remains combined with substantial wild-resource use.

## 6.5 Domestication-intensification phase

Human selection increasingly shapes managed populations and production commitments.

## 6.6 Settlement-investment phase

Storage, fields, structures, and institutions increase attachment to place.

## 6.7 Agrarian-dependence phase

Food production becomes central enough that population and institutions depend on it.

These phases may overlap, reverse, or occur in different sequences.

# 7. Environmental Conditions

## 7.1 Resource abundance

Abundant wild resources can reduce pressure to cultivate, but may also support sedentism and experimentation.

## 7.2 Resource predictability

Predictable seasonal resources make scheduling and repeated management easier.

## 7.3 Patch concentration

Concentrated resources reduce search cost and can support aggregation sites.

## 7.4 Water reliability

Reliable water supports longer occupation, processing, animals, and cultivation.

## 7.5 Soil workability

Soils influence labour needs, drainage, water retention, and response to disturbance.

## 7.6 Disturbance ecology

Fire, flood, grazing, and clearing can increase some useful resources while reducing others.

## 7.7 Climate variability

High variability can encourage diversification, mobility, storage, or stronger control.

## 7.8 Ecotonal opportunity

Boundaries between ecosystems can provide diverse resources and experimentation opportunities.

---

# 8. Seasonality and Resource Scheduling

## 8.1 Annual calendars

Communities sequence collection, hunting, fishing, processing, movement, planting, harvesting, and storage.

## 8.2 Food gaps

Predictable shortage periods increase the value of storage, exchange, and early production.

## 8.3 Labour conflicts

Different tasks may compete for labour at the same time.

## 8.4 Risk staggering

Different timing reduces the chance that all resources fail together.

## 8.5 Seasonal aggregation

People may gather at rich seasonal sites without becoming fully sedentary.

## 8.6 Calendar knowledge

Observation of stars, weather, plant stages, animal movement, and water helps schedule action.

---

# 9. Broad-Spectrum Subsistence

## 9.1 Dietary diversification

Using many foods reduces reliance on one source.

## 9.2 Processing expansion

New tools make previously costly or difficult foods usable.

## 9.3 Small-resource use

Lower-ranked resources may become worthwhile when population or scarcity increases.

## 9.4 Habitat diversification

Communities exploit multiple environments and seasonal zones.

## 9.5 Knowledge complexity

A broad diet requires extensive classification, timing, processing, and safety knowledge.

## 9.6 Transition potential

Frequent use of certain resources can create opportunities for management and selection.

---

# 10. Landscape Management

## 10.1 Controlled fire

Fire can clear vegetation, stimulate regrowth, concentrate animals, and favour selected plants.

## 10.2 Selective protection

Useful plants or habitats may be protected from cutting, grazing, or disturbance.

## 10.3 Transplanting

Useful organisms may be moved closer to camps or favourable locations.

## 10.4 Water modification

Small channels, barriers, pits, or retention structures alter local water availability.

## 10.5 Soil disturbance

Digging and clearing can unintentionally or intentionally favour certain species.

## 10.6 Harvest restraint

Rules may limit timing or intensity to preserve future reproduction.

## 10.7 Animal movement control

Corralling, driving, seasonal access, or protection can change animal behaviour and selection.

---

# 11. Cultivation Before Domestication

## 11.1 Sowing wild material

Wild-type seed or propagules may be deliberately planted before domesticated traits exist.

## 11.2 Tending natural stands

Weeding, watering, pruning, or guarding increases returns without full domestication.

## 11.3 Harvest selection

Methods of harvesting influence which traits reproduce.

## 11.4 Seed retention

Retaining material from preferred plants creates repeated cultural selection.

## 11.5 Field protection

Fencing, watch, or social rules protect delayed-return investment.

## 11.6 Trial plots

Small experimental areas allow comparison with wild acquisition.

## 11.7 Learning cycles

Observation across seasons improves timing, spacing, and site selection.

---

# 12. Domestication Processes

## 12.1 Unconscious selection

Routine harvesting and replanting can alter traits without explicit breeding goals.

## 12.2 Conscious selection

People deliberately choose size, taste, ease of harvest, behaviour, timing, or storage traits.

## 12.3 Reproductive control

Human influence over mating, seed retention, movement, or isolation increases selection.

## 12.4 Protection from natural pressures

Managed populations may survive under traits that would be disadvantaged in the wild.

## 12.5 Population bottlenecks

Repeated use of limited breeding material reduces diversity.

## 12.6 Co-evolution

Human institutions and managed organisms change in response to each other.

## 12.7 Domestication lag

Cultivation or management may persist for long periods before clear domesticated traits appear.

## 12.8 Reversal and escape

Managed populations can return to less controlled conditions or mix with wild populations.

---

# 13. Sedentism and Settlement

## 13.1 Resource-based sedentism

Rich, predictable wild resources can support long occupation before agriculture.

## 13.2 Storage-based sedentism

Heavy stores and facilities increase attachment to place.

## 13.3 Field-based sedentism

Land preparation and protection require repeated return.

## 13.4 Infrastructure-based sedentism

Wells, houses, paths, and processing facilities represent sunk investment.

## 13.5 Social sedentism

Burial, ritual, kinship, and identity strengthen place attachment.

## 13.6 Costs of permanence

Waste, disease, conflict, fuel depletion, and local resource pressure can rise.

## 13.7 Partial permanence

Some people or activities remain mobile while others stay at a main settlement.

---

# 14. Storage and Delayed Returns

## 14.1 Preservation prerequisite

Food must often be dried, cleaned, sealed, cooled, fermented, or otherwise stabilized.

## 14.2 Capacity planning

Reserve size depends on population, food gaps, risk, seed needs, and expected loss.

## 14.3 Ownership

Storage raises questions about household, communal, institutional, or elite control.

## 14.4 Security

Stores attract pests, theft, conflict, and political extraction.

## 14.5 Rotation

Older stock must be consumed before unacceptable quality loss.

## 14.6 Seed separation

Planting reserves require protection from consumption and mixing.

## 14.7 Information

Stored quantity, age, quality, ownership, and obligation must be remembered or recorded.

## 14.8 Release rules

Communities need rules for ordinary use, emergency use, redistribution, and repayment.

---

# 15. Labour Organization

## 15.1 Household labour

Production commonly begins through household allocation and task specialization.

## 15.2 Reciprocal labour

Households exchange work during peak demand.

## 15.3 Communal labour

Shared infrastructure or fields require coordinated group work.

## 15.4 Task specialization

Seed selection, water control, storage construction, ritual timing, or tool making may become specialized.

## 15.5 Gender and age organization

Tasks may be allocated by age, gender, skill, status, or mobility.

## 15.6 Labour coercion risk

Control of land and surplus can produce dependent or coerced labour.

## 15.7 Peak demand

Planting and harvest often create short periods when delay causes major loss.

## 15.8 Maintenance burden

Fields, fences, water systems, storage, and tools create continuing obligations.

---

# 16. Property, Access, and Territoriality

## 16.1 Use rights

A person or group may have rights to use without full ownership.

## 16.2 Seasonal rights

Access may change by season, resource, or activity.

## 16.3 Investment claims

Labour invested in fields or storage strengthens expectations of future benefit.

## 16.4 Inheritance

Delayed-return assets create questions about transfer across generations.

## 16.5 Common resources

Water, pasture, forest, and routes may remain shared under rules.

## 16.6 Exclusion

Stronger claims can restrict earlier open access.

## 16.7 Boundary formation

Fields, paths, fences, markers, and memory make rights more visible.

## 16.8 Dispute resolution

Councils, custom, ritual, compensation, or force may resolve conflict.

---

# 17. Surplus and Exchange

## 17.1 True surplus

Surplus exists only after consumption, seed, feed, obligations, and realistic losses.

## 17.2 Buffer surplus

Reserve held for uncertainty rather than exchange.

## 17.3 Prestige surplus

Food used for feasting, alliance, ritual, or status.

## 17.4 Exchange surplus

Output transferred for tools, materials, services, credit, or relationships.

## 17.5 Political surplus

Output appropriated through tribute, rent, taxation, or authority.

## 17.6 Measurement

Surplus exchange increases demand for accepted units and quality rules.

## 17.7 Credit

Delayed settlement of value creates obligations and trust systems.

## 17.8 Redistribution

Central collection may support resilience or strengthen hierarchy.

---

# 18. Population and Carrying Capacity

## 18.1 Demand effect

More people increase total food needs.

## 18.2 Labour effect

More people can provide more workers, knowledge, and defence.

## 18.3 Density effect

Higher density can reduce mobility and increase competition.

## 18.4 Disease effect

Permanent dense settlements increase transmission risk.

## 18.5 Effective carrying capacity

Cultivation, storage, exchange, and infrastructure can raise supported population.

## 18.6 Overshoot

Population may exceed sustainable capacity during favourable periods.

## 18.7 Feedback

Higher production can support growth that creates pressure for further intensification.

# 19. Technology and Tools

## 19.1 Digging tools

Increase the ability to disturb soil, collect underground foods, and prepare planting sites.

## 19.2 Cutting tools

Support clearing, harvesting, woodworking, and construction.

## 19.3 Grinding tools

Reduce processing cost and expand usable foods.

## 19.4 Containers

Enable transport, soaking, fermentation, storage, and measurement.

## 19.5 Fire technology

Supports cooking, preservation, landscape management, and protection.

## 19.6 Water-control tools

Enable local irrigation, drainage, retention, or lifting.

## 19.7 Fencing and barriers

Protect fields, animals, stores, and access claims.

## 19.8 Transport devices

Increase movement of bulk food, water, tools, and construction material.

## 19.9 Recording devices

Tallies, marks, tokens, and later writing support obligation and inventory memory.

# 21. Metrics and KPIs

- **Code:** `wild_food_share`
- **Unit or expression:** % of diet
- **Meaning:** Estimated share of food obtained from unmanaged or lightly managed wild resources.

# 24. Decision Trees and Logic Rules

## 24.1 Adopt cultivation

IF a useful resource is predictable enough to manage AND labour is available AND delayed benefits exceed opportunity cost THEN begin or expand cultivation.

## 24.2 Maintain mixed strategy

IF cultivated output remains uncertain OR wild resources remain abundant THEN retain a mixed subsistence portfolio.

## 24.3 Increase sedentism

IF local food, water, storage, and social security support longer occupation THEN extend settlement duration.

## 24.4 Return to mobility

IF local resources decline OR conflict rises OR disease pressure increases THEN restore seasonal movement or relocate.

## 24.5 Store surplus

IF production or collection is seasonal AND future shortage is expected THEN allocate labour to preservation and storage.

## 24.6 Reduce storage concentration

IF centralized reserves create excessive theft, spoilage, or political risk THEN distribute stores across households or locations.

## 24.7 Select planting material

IF a specimen shows preferred traits AND remains viable THEN retain it separately for future reproduction.

## 24.8 Expand field area

IF labour, water, seed, protection, and storage exceed current needs THEN increase cultivated area.

## 24.9 Intensify existing area

IF land expansion is constrained BUT labour and management can increase output THEN intensify current plots.

## 24.10 Abandon trial

IF repeated cultivation returns remain below wild-resource alternatives after learning effects are considered THEN reduce or abandon the trial.

## 24.11 Formalize access

IF investment in fields or storage increases conflict over use THEN create stronger access, inheritance, or exclusion rules.

## 24.12 Coordinate communal labour

IF a task exceeds household capacity AND benefits are shared THEN mobilize reciprocal or collective labour.

## 24.13 Diversify timing

IF one harvest window creates high failure risk THEN stagger planting, collection, or storage strategies.

## 24.14 Protect seed reserve

IF food shortage threatens planting material THEN separate and socially protect seed reserves from consumption.

## 24.15 Exchange for resilience

IF another group has uncorrelated resources or seasons THEN strengthen exchange ties to reduce shared risk.

# 26. Risks

## 26.1 Climate instability

Rapid or repeated changes in temperature, rainfall, seasonality, flood, drought, or storm patterns.

## 26.2 Resource depletion

Overharvesting, overhunting, repeated burning, or excessive grazing reduces future availability.

## 26.3 Settlement disease

Higher density and waste accumulation increase exposure to infectious disease and parasites.

## 26.4 Storage loss

Moisture, pests, rodents, mould, theft, fire, or structural failure destroy reserves.

## 26.5 Labour overload

Cultivation adds tasks to existing gathering, hunting, fishing, child care, processing, and social duties.

## 26.6 Seed loss

Planting material is consumed, spoiled, mixed, stolen, or loses viability.

## 26.7 Conflict over access

Fields, water, storage, or managed patches create disputes over rights and exclusion.

## 26.8 Selection error

Preferred visible traits are selected even when they reduce resilience or future performance.

## 26.9 Ecological simplification

Dependence on fewer resources increases vulnerability to shared failure.

## 26.10 Social inequality

Control of fields, stores, animals, or labour becomes concentrated.

## 26.11 Path dependence

Earlier investments make continued cultivation necessary even when returns decline.

## 26.12 Knowledge interruption

Migration, mortality, conflict, or social disruption breaks transmission.

## 26.13 Nutritional narrowing

Increased staple dependence reduces dietary diversity.

## 26.14 Exchange breakdown

Loss of allies or routes removes an important buffer against local shortage.

## 26.15 Predation and raiding

Stored surplus attracts theft, conflict, or political extraction.

# 27. Failure Modes and Loss Points

## 27.1 Premature sedentism

Settlement becomes more permanent before local food, sanitation, water, or storage can support it.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.2 Overcommitment to cultivation

Labour moves away from diverse wild resources before production becomes reliable.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.3 Seed reserve consumption

Short-term hunger destroys the next production cycle.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.4 Storage without preservation

Food is stored before adequate drying, cleaning, or protection.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.5 Property conflict

Investment rises faster than shared rules for access and inheritance.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.6 Labour-calendar collision

Planting, harvesting, gathering, hunting, and social obligations peak at the same time.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.7 Resource-patch degradation

Management repeatedly favours immediate yield over regeneration.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.8 Domestication bottleneck

A narrow selected population loses diversity and resilience.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.9 Water-control failure

Simple irrigation or drainage creates erosion, salinity, waterlogging, or conflict.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.10 Centralized reserve capture

Authorities or raiders seize stores, leaving households exposed.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.11 Knowledge monopoly

Critical techniques become concentrated in too few custodians.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.12 Rigid seasonal assumptions

Traditional timing fails under changing climate but institutions resist adjustment.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.13 Transport overload

Bulk production exceeds carrying, road, animal, or vessel capacity.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.14 Waste concentration

Permanent settlement creates sanitation and pest problems.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

## 27.15 False surplus

Output appears abundant but obligations, seed needs, losses, and food gaps are underestimated.

**Typical indicators:** declining diversity, reserve depletion, conflict, rising labour, repeated failure, abandonment, or forced migration.

# 34. Historiography

## 34.1 Single-revolution model

Treats agriculture as a rapid transformation; useful for broad comparison but often hides long transitional phases.

## 34.2 Gradualist model

Emphasizes cumulative management, experimentation, and mixed subsistence over long periods.

## 34.3 Population-pressure model

Explains change partly through rising demand, while requiring evidence that alternatives were insufficient.

## 34.4 Climate-opportunity model

Emphasizes environmental stabilization or change that altered resource predictability.

## 34.5 Risk-management model

Views cultivation and storage as strategies for reducing uncertainty rather than maximizing average output.

## 34.6 Feasting and social-competition model

Emphasizes prestige, ritual, alliance-building, and demand for surplus.

## 34.7 Niche-construction model

Focuses on reciprocal change between human management and evolving environments.

## 34.8 Political-economy model

Examines labour, property, surplus control, hierarchy, and institutional power.

## 34.9 Multi-pathway model

Rejects a single global sequence and emphasizes different combinations of ecology, culture, exchange, and demography.

## 34.10 Co-evolutionary model

Explains domestication as mutual change among humans, organisms, institutions, and landscapes.
