# 1. Purpose of This Training Asset

This training asset introduces two of the oldest human food-acquisition strategies:

1. early plant gathering;
2. early animal hunting.

These strategies existed long before farming. They formed the foundation of human subsistence, ecological knowledge, mobility, social cooperation, tool use, food sharing, and later experimentation with cultivation and domestication.

The purpose of this document is not to present a simple story in which humans first hunted, then gathered, and finally invented agriculture. Human subsistence was more varied than that. Different groups relied on different combinations of plants, animals, fish, shellfish, insects, honey, eggs, and scavenged resources depending on local ecology, climate, technology, season, risk, and knowledge.

This document therefore trains NexVox Studio L2 to understand that:

- gathering and hunting often occurred together;
- neither activity had one universal form;
- plant foods may be underrepresented in archaeology because they preserve poorly;
- animal bones and stone tools often preserve better than fruits, leaves, roots, and wooden implements;
- human diets changed across environments and periods;
- agriculture emerged gradually from long traditions of observation, selection, harvesting, processing, storage, and landscape knowledge.

# 3. Essential Definitions

## 3.1 Foraging

Foraging is the acquisition of food from wild environments.

It includes:

- gathering plants;
- collecting fruits and nuts;
- digging roots and tubers;
- collecting shellfish;
- fishing;
- trapping;
- scavenging;
- hunting;
- collecting eggs;
- collecting insects;
- collecting honey.

Foraging is broader than hunting.

## 3.2 Plant Gathering

Plant gathering is the intentional search for, identification of, collection of, transport of, processing of, and consumption of wild plant resources.

Gathering may involve:

- fruits;
- seeds;
- grains;
- nuts;
- roots;
- tubers;
- bulbs;
- leaves;
- stems;
- flowers;
- gums;
- resins;
- bark;
- fungi;
- medicinal plants;
- fibers.

Gathering requires knowledge. A gatherer must know which species are edible, poisonous, seasonal, medicinal, fibrous, storable, or useful for tools.

## 3.3 Hunting

Hunting is the intentional pursuit, capture, or killing of wild animals.

Hunting can involve:

- direct pursuit;
- ambush;
- cooperative drives;
- trapping;
- spearing;
- throwing projectiles;
- clubbing;
- netting;
- fishing;
- bird capture;
- egg collection;
- small-animal capture.

## 3.4 Scavenging

Scavenging is the acquisition of animal tissue from carcasses not killed by the consumer.

Possible sources include:

- natural deaths;
- abandoned predator kills;
- animals trapped by environmental events;
- carcasses accessed after competing with predators.

Scavenging can be passive or confrontational.

## 3.5 Hunter-Gatherer

A hunter-gatherer is a person or member of a society whose subsistence relies mainly on wild resources rather than domesticated crops and livestock.

This term should not imply that hunting was always more important than gathering.

## 3.6 Subsistence

Subsistence refers to the methods by which people obtain the food, water, shelter materials, and other resources needed to live.

## 3.7 Broad-Spectrum Foraging

Broad-spectrum foraging is the use of many different resource types rather than dependence on one narrow food source.

A broad-spectrum diet may include:

- grains;
- fruits;
- nuts;
- roots;
- fish;
- shellfish;
- birds;
- small mammals;
- large game;
- insects;
- honey.

## 3.8 Archaeobotany

Archaeobotany is the study of plant remains from archaeological sites.

Evidence may include:

- charred seeds;
- pollen;
- phytoliths;
- starch grains;
- plant fibers;
- wood charcoal;
- residues on tools;
- dental calculus;
- preserved plant tissue.

## 3.9 Zooarchaeology

Zooarchaeology is the study of animal remains from archaeological sites.

Evidence may include:

- bones;
- teeth;
- antler;
- shell;
- cut marks;
- burning;
- fracture patterns;
- species profiles;
- age profiles;
- body-part representation.

# 4. Historical and Evolutionary Context

Humans and earlier hominins evolved as flexible omnivores.

Their foods varied according to:

- environment;
- latitude;
- rainfall;
- temperature;
- season;
- water access;
- local plant communities;
- animal migrations;
- available tools;
- social organization;
- learned traditions.

There was no single universal prehistoric diet.

Some environments offered abundant fruits, nuts, roots, and leafy plants.

Other environments offered fewer plant foods but more fish, marine resources, or large mammals.

Some groups moved frequently.

Others returned seasonally to the same places.

Some developed storage.

Some occupied semi-permanent camps.

The history of subsistence is therefore a history of adaptation.

# PART I — EARLY PLANT GATHERING

# 5. Why Plant Gathering Matters

Plant gathering was likely one of the most continuous food-acquisition activities in human history.

Plant foods could provide:

- carbohydrates;
- fats;
- vitamins;
- minerals;
- fiber;
- water;
- flavor;
- medicine;
- fuel;
- materials.

Unlike large-game hunting, gathering often produced smaller but more predictable returns.

A patch of fruiting trees, edible tubers, nut-bearing plants, or seed grasses could be revisited.

The returns depended on:

- season;
- rainfall;
- competition;
- plant density;
- processing time;
- travel distance;
- storage potential.

Gathering was not automatically easy. Many plants required extensive processing before they were safe or useful.

Examples of processing include:

- peeling;
- pounding;
- grinding;
- soaking;
- roasting;
- leaching;
- drying;
- cracking;
- winnowing;
- boiling;
- fermenting.

---

# 6. Categories of Gathered Plant Foods

## 6.1 Fruits

Wild fruits could provide:

- sugars;
- water;
- vitamins;
- seeds;
- fibers.

Fruits were often seasonal.

Gatherers needed to know:

- when fruit ripened;
- which animals competed for it;
- which colors indicated ripeness;
- which fruits caused illness;
- which fruits could be dried;
- which fruits attracted dangerous animals.

## 6.2 Nuts

Nuts could provide:

- fats;
- protein;
- calories;
- storage potential.

Many nuts required:

- shell cracking;
- roasting;
- grinding;
- leaching to remove bitterness or toxins.

Nuts were important because fat-rich foods could be valuable where diets were otherwise lean.

## 6.3 Seeds and Wild Grains

Wild seeds and grasses could be gathered by:

- hand picking;
- beating seed heads into containers;
- stripping stalks;
- sweeping fallen seeds;
- harvesting mature stands.

Processing could include:

- drying;
- threshing;
- winnowing;
- grinding;
- mixing with water;
- cooking.

Repeated harvesting of selected seed stands helped create knowledge later relevant to cultivation.

## 6.4 Roots, Tubers, Bulbs, and Rhizomes

Underground storage organs could be rich in starch.

They often required:

- digging sticks;
- knowledge of hidden plant locations;
- identification from leaves or stems;
- roasting;
- pounding;
- detoxification.

These resources could remain available when above-ground fruits were absent.

## 6.5 Leaves and Stems

Leaves and young stems could provide nutrients and moisture.

Gatherers needed to distinguish:

- edible species;
- medicinal species;
- irritants;
- poisonous look-alikes;
- seasonally edible growth stages.

## 6.6 Flowers, Nectar, and Honey

Flowers could provide nectar, pollen, and edible plant parts.

Honey provided concentrated energy but collecting it could involve:

- climbing;
- smoke;
- protective knowledge;
- cooperation;
- risk of stings or falls.

## 6.7 Fungi

Mushrooms and other fungi could provide food, medicine, or toxins.

Reliable identification required deep local knowledge because edible and poisonous species may resemble one another.

## 6.8 Gums, Resins, and Sap

Tree gums, resins, and sap could be:

- eaten;
- chewed;
- used medicinally;
- used as adhesives;
- used in fire-making;
- used in tool manufacture.

## 6.9 Aquatic Plants

Wetlands could provide:

- roots;
- reeds;
- seeds;
- tubers;
- edible stems.

Aquatic landscapes often supported mixed subsistence strategies involving plants, fish, shellfish, birds, and mammals.

This knowledge could be encoded in:

It included:

1. locating;

8. sharing;
9. teaching.

# 8. Seasonality

Seasonality is the predictable variation of resources through the year.

A food calendar might include:

- early rains;
- flowering season;
- fruiting season;
- dry-season tubers;
- nut season;
- migratory-animal season;
- river-fishing season.

Seasonality affected:

- camp location;
- group size;
- travel;
- storage;
- trade;
- risk;
- labor allocation;
- social gatherings.

A single landscape could support different resources at different times.

Understanding these cycles was essential to survival.

# 9. Mobility and Plant Gathering

Mobility was often linked to resource distribution.

People might:

- move camp;
- make short collection trips;
- send specialized groups;
- revisit known patches;
- establish seasonal camps;
- maintain territories;
- exchange access with neighboring groups.

Mobility should not be understood as aimless wandering.

It could be highly organized and predictable.

Routes might connect:

- water;
- fruit;
- tubers;
- game;
- stone sources;
- shelter;
- ritual sites.

# 10. Tools Used in Plant Gathering

## 10.1 Digging Sticks

Digging sticks could be used to:

- loosen soil;
- extract tubers;
- dig small animals;
- investigate roots;
- plant or encourage useful species.

Wooden tools preserve poorly, so their archaeological visibility may be limited.

## 10.2 Baskets

Baskets could transport:

- fruits;
- nuts;
- seeds;
- tubers;
- firewood;
- infants;
- tools.

Plant-fiber containers rarely survive, but impressions and rare preserved examples show their importance.

## 10.3 Carrying Nets and Bags

Nets and bags could be made from:

- bark fiber;
- grass;
- reeds;
- sinew;
- hide.

Containers greatly increased the amount of food that could be moved.

## 10.4 Grinding Stones

Grinding stones could process:

- seeds;
- grains;
- nuts;
- pigments;
- medicines.

Grinding increased digestibility and allowed new food forms.

## 10.5 Mortars and Pestles

Pounding could:

- break shells;
- crush fibers;
- process seeds;
- reduce cooking time;
- prepare mixed foods.

## 10.6 Cutting Tools

Stone flakes could cut:

- stems;
- bark;
- roots;
- fruits;
- fibers.

## 10.7 Fire

Fire could:

- roast tubers;
- detoxify plants;
- soften fibers;
- improve digestibility;
- preserve food;
- alter landscapes;
- encourage selected species.

# 11. Processing and Cooking

Many wild plants are not immediately edible.

Processing can:

- reduce toxins;
- improve taste;
- release nutrients;
- break tough fibers;
- kill pathogens;
- reduce chewing effort;
- extend storage life.

Common methods include:

## 11.1 Roasting

Roasting could soften roots and tubers.

## 11.2 Boiling

Boiling may require containers or heated stones.

## 11.3 Grinding

Grinding creates flour or paste.

## 11.4 Leaching

Leaching removes bitter or toxic compounds.

## 11.5 Drying

Drying reduces moisture and delays spoilage.

## 11.6 Fermentation

Fermentation changes flavor, preservation, and digestibility.

# 12. Storage

Storage changes the relationship between people and food.

Without storage, people must obtain food frequently.

With storage, people can buffer:

- bad weather;
- seasonal gaps;
- hunting failure;
- illness;
- travel disruption.

Stored foods may include:

- nuts;
- seeds;
- dried fruits;
- roots;
- flour;
- smoked foods.

Storage may encourage:

- longer occupation;
- territorial defense;
- property claims;
- planning;
- delayed consumption;
- social inequality in later contexts.

Storage is one bridge between mobile foraging and more settled life.

# 16. Archaeological Evidence for Plant Gathering

Plant gathering is difficult to reconstruct because many remains decay.

Archaeologists use multiple evidence types.

## 16.1 Charred Seeds

Seeds may survive when burned.

## 16.2 Pollen

Pollen can indicate local vegetation but not always direct consumption.

## 16.3 Phytoliths

Phytoliths are microscopic mineral structures formed in plants.

## 16.4 Starch Grains

Starch may survive on tools or teeth.

## 16.5 Dental Calculus

Mineralized plaque can trap microscopic plant residues.

## 16.6 Grinding Stones

Wear and residues can indicate plant processing.

## 16.7 Storage Pits

Pits may contain charred seeds or plant remains.

## 16.8 Coprolites

Preserved feces can reveal diet.

## 16.9 Isotopes

Chemical signatures in bones and teeth can provide broad dietary information.

No single method gives a complete answer.

---

# 17. Why Plant Foods Are Often Underestimated

Plant evidence is often underrepresented because:

- leaves decay;
- fruits decay;
- roots decay;
- baskets decay;
- wooden tools decay;
- processing may leave few visible traces;
- animal bones preserve better;
- stone hunting tools are visually striking;
- older research sometimes focused more heavily on hunting.

NexVox Studio should therefore avoid assuming that absence of preserved plant remains means absence of plant use.

---

# 18. Gathering and the Path Toward Cultivation

Plant gathering created knowledge later useful for cultivation.

Repeated gathering could teach people:

- where plants grow;
- when seeds mature;
- how rainfall affects growth;
- how fire changes vegetation;
- which seeds are larger;
- which plants are easier to process;
- which patches are productive;
- how disturbed soil affects germination.

Possible intermediate behaviors include:

- protecting useful plants;
- clearing competing vegetation;
- burning selected areas;
- scattering seeds;
- transplanting;
- watering;
- selective harvesting;
- storing seed.

These actions do not automatically equal agriculture.

They represent a continuum between gathering and cultivation.

# 20. Scavenging Before or Alongside Hunting

Early access to meat may have included scavenging.

Scavenging could involve:

- finding natural deaths;
- accessing predator kills;
- arriving after large carnivores;
- breaking bones for marrow;
- cutting remaining tissue;
- confronting or driving away predators.

Archaeologists examine:

- tooth marks;
- cut marks;
- bone breakage;
- body-part representation;
- carnivore damage;
- tool marks.

The sequence of marks can help reconstruct whether humans accessed carcasses early or late.

Hunting and scavenging are not mutually exclusive.

A group could do both.

# 38. Gender and Hunting

Prehistoric hunting should not be described using rigid universal gender rules.

# PART III — INTEGRATED UNDERSTANDING

# 41. Gathering and Hunting as Complementary Strategies

Gathering and hunting are best understood as complementary.

Gathering may offer:

- predictable daily returns;
- plant carbohydrates;
- vitamins;
- storable foods;
- childcare compatibility in some settings.

Hunting may offer:

- concentrated protein;
- fat;
- hides;
- bones;
- high social value;
- large shared returns.

A mixed strategy reduces dependence on one resource.

---

# 42. Energy Return and Risk

A food-acquisition decision involves trade-offs.

A simplified reasoning model is:

```text
Expected value =
probability of success
× nutritional return
− travel cost
− processing cost
− injury risk
− opportunity cost
```

This is not a literal formula early people calculated numerically.

It is a modern analytical model.

Gathering may have lower variance.

Large-game hunting may have higher variance.

Subistence strategies differ across:

- forests;
- grasslands;
- coasts;

- mountains;

- wetlands;
- cold environments.

Therefore:

- one site cannot represent all humans;
- one modern foraging society cannot represent all prehistory;

# 44. Evidence and Uncertainty

NexVox Studio must distinguish:

## 44.1 Direct Evidence

Examples:

- a preserved seed;
- a cut-marked bone;
- a spear;
- a grinding stone.

## 44.2 Indirect Evidence

Examples:

- isotopic patterns;
- tool wear;
- residue;
- landscape change.

## 44.3 Ethnographic Analogy

Modern or recent societies may help interpret possibilities.

Analogy is useful but not proof.

## 44.4 Experimental Archaeology

Researchers reproduce tools or tasks to test hypotheses.

## 44.5 Speculation

Speculation must be labeled.

# 45. Common Misconceptions

## Misconception 1

Early humans ate mostly meat everywhere.

### Correction

Diet varied by ecology and time. Plant foods were often important but preserve poorly.

## Misconception 2

Gathering required little skill.

### Correction

Gathering required detailed ecological knowledge, processing techniques, and risk management.

## Misconception 3

Hunting began as organized large-game hunting.

### Correction

Animal acquisition likely included scavenging, opportunistic capture, small game, fishing, and many hunting methods.

## Misconception 4

Men always hunted and women always gathered.

### Correction

Labor patterns varied. Evidence does not support a universal rule.

## Misconception 5

Agriculture appeared suddenly.

### Correction

Agriculture emerged through long processes of observation, selection, harvesting, storage, cultivation, and domestication.

# 46. Timeline Framework

The following timeline is approximate and should not be treated as a single global sequence.

## Before the Genus Homo

Earlier hominins used mixed plant and animal resources.

## Early Homo

Stone tools increased access to animal tissue and plant processing.

## Homo erectus and Related Populations

Mobility, hunting, scavenging, and tool use expanded.

## Middle Pleistocene

More evidence appears for planned hunting, wooden spears, and organized carcass processing.

## Late Pleistocene

Many populations used broad-spectrum foraging, fishing, plant processing, storage, and specialized tools.

## Terminal Pleistocene and Early Holocene

Some groups intensified plant harvesting, storage, semi-sedentary life, and selective resource use.

## Early Agriculture

Cultivation and domestication emerged independently in several regions.

# 51. Training Rules for NexVox Studio L2

NexVox Studio must:

1. avoid presenting one universal prehistoric diet;
2. distinguish evidence from inference;
3. identify preservation bias;
4. describe gathering as skilled ecological practice;
5. distinguish scavenging, hunting, fishing, trapping, and collecting;
6. avoid rigid gender stereotypes;
7. preserve chronological uncertainty;
8. explain regional variation;
9. link gathering to cultivation without claiming a simple direct path;
10. link hunting to animal management without claiming all hunted animals became domesticates;
11. cite sources when producing factual historical explanations;
12. label modern analytical formulas as models rather than prehistoric calculations.

# 52. Summary

Early plant gathering and early animal hunting were foundational human subsistence strategies.

Plant gathering required:

- ecological knowledge;
- seasonal planning;
- species identification;
- processing;
- containers;
- mobility;
- teaching.

Animal acquisition required:

- scavenging;
- tracking;
- weapons;
- cooperation;
- risk management;
- butchery;
- sharing.

Together, these strategies built knowledge of plants, animals, landscapes, seasons, food preservation, and social organization.

That accumulated knowledge created the preconditions for later cultivation, domestication, settlement, and agriculture.
