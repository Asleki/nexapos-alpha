# Purpose

This volume introduces named continents, historical regions, peoples, governments, states, trade systems, and intercontinental relationships during agricultural evolution.

It remains a bridge volume. It names historical places and political systems but does not yet provide detailed country histories, crop histories, or technical farming-method histories.

# 1. Scope and Boundaries

## 1.1 Included

- named continents
- named historical regions
- named pre-modern states, kingdoms, empires, and polities
- broad social groups and historical peoples
- migration, trade, diplomacy, conflict, and institutional links
- agricultural governance, storage, taxation, land, and labor

## 1.2 Excluded

- detailed crop histories
- technical production methods
- modern national policy
- contemporary institutions
- detailed country-by-country agricultural profiles

## 1.3 Historical Boundary

The emphasis is on pre-industrial and early global-commercial agricultural evolution. Later industrial and contemporary systems belong in later volumes.

# 2. Historical Period Framework

## 2.1 Early settlement formation

Small settlements, kinship authority, local exchange, and gradual investment in land and storage.

## 2.2 Regional chiefdom development

Larger political territories, tribute, ceremonial centers, and stronger control over labor and surplus.

## 2.3 Early state consolidation

Administrative roles, taxation, records, infrastructure, and standing political institutions.

## 2.4 Kingdom and empire expansion

Roads, armies, tribute, resettlement, and provincial governance connect wider agrarian regions.

## 2.5 Interregional trade intensification

Merchants, ports, caravan centers, market towns, and standardized measures increase exchange.

## 2.6 Transcontinental integration

Large land and maritime networks connect distant regions through trade, migration, religion, and war.

## 2.7 Colonial restructuring

External states reorganize land, labor, trade, taxation, settlement, and legal systems.

## 2.8 Early global commercial transition

Oceanic routes, empires, finance, and mass commodity exchange integrate continental economies before industrial modernity.

# 3. Africa

## 3.1 Continental Overview

Africa contained multiple ecological, linguistic, political, and social histories. Agricultural evolution differed by environment, population movement, governance, and connection to wider trade networks.

## 3.2 Nile Valley and adjacent northeast

River-based settlement, floodplain coordination, storage administration, and long-distance exchange linked the interior, coast, and neighboring regions.

## 3.3 Sahel and savanna belts

Seasonal movement, mixed livelihoods, caravan exchange, and political control of routes connected inland communities across wide ecological gradients.

## 3.4 Horn and Red Sea interface

Highlands, lowlands, maritime contact, and corridor trade connected inland producers with coastal and overseas exchange systems.

## 3.5 West African forest–savanna transition

Ecological boundaries supported exchange between communities specializing in different resources, settlement patterns, and political forms.

## 3.6 Central African forest zones

River movement, dispersed settlement, forest knowledge, and gradual migration shaped agricultural expansion and cultural interaction.

## 3.7 Great Lakes and eastern highlands

Highland basins, lake corridors, cattle-keeping communities, farming settlements, and regional exchange created dense networks.

## 3.8 Southern African plateau and river systems

Migration, cattle wealth, settlement expansion, mining links, and regional chiefdoms tied agriculture to political consolidation.

# 26. Historical Decision Logic

## 26.1 Route choice

IF a route offers water, security, markets, and acceptable cost THEN traders and migrants are more likely to use it.

## 26.2 State storage

IF taxation produces regular surplus AND administration can secure it THEN centralized storage expands.

## 26.3 Frontier settlement

IF rulers seek defense, tax, or land control THEN they may encourage or compel settlement.

## 26.4 Migration

IF local risk exceeds expected opportunity elsewhere THEN households or communities may move.

## 26.5 Alliance trade

IF trust and repeated exchange lower transaction risk THEN long-distance networks strengthen.

## 26.6 Tax resistance

IF extraction threatens survival or legitimacy THEN evasion, migration, revolt, or negotiation becomes more likely.

## 26.7 Port growth

IF maritime routes, inland access, political security, and demand align THEN port settlements expand.

## 26.8 Empire integration

IF roads, officials, military force, and taxation remain effective THEN diverse agrarian regions can be governed together.

## 26.9 Knowledge adoption

IF incoming institutions or practices fit local conditions and incentives THEN adoption becomes more likely.

## 26.10 Institutional decline

IF conflict, fiscal stress, route disruption, and legitimacy loss combine THEN political control over agriculture weakens.
